Electricity Demand Analysis: Insights into Unmapped Factors for Reliable Forecast during Uncertainty

Let’s start understanding how the electricity demand has changed during the COVID period in Australia. For our analysis we have selected the top 2 COVID hotspots i.e. Victoria and NSW. Our prediction model trained from 1/1/2016 to 30/12/2019 shows that there is a modest change in the demand for electricity in Victoria and much higher change observed in NSW. The average percentage change (predicted – actual) from March – Aug 2020 for Victoria shows a decline of 2.1% while this percentage is slightly higher for NSW region equal to 2.4%. High demand change can be attributed to stricter restrictions in NSW (reasoning). Although the percentage was expected to be significant due to shut down of the commercial and industrial activity, but much of the change seems to be offset by high residential consumption as people have started working from home. Factors represent temporal change

To interpret the changes in electricity demand, we start by investigating two major factors, namely social distancing and public health. In Victoria, we used Melbourne Pedestrian count data to represent social distancing while in New South Wales, the same factor is represented by Google trend data of keywords related to daily outdoor activities performed by people. The keywords we choose are; ‘Train’, ‘Event’, and ‘hotel’. For instance; the graph shows variations in time scales of different factors influencing electricity consumption during COVID -19 pandemic. It is apparent that electricity consumption started dropping almost immediately the government put COVID related restrictions in place. This is seen by people’s changing behaviour represented by their google search behaviour. Following this, the number of new confirmed cases started increasing rapidly with a major spike observed on 3 March 2020. It is also observed that electricity demand started rising almost immediately after decline in number of cases and ease of restricting.

The above factors represent different temporal changes. For instance, in NSW, fig 2, electricity demand shows an early decline with respect to increase in the number of confirmed cases of COVID-19. Further, electricity demand showcased a delayed rebound in comparison to social distance measures with respect to increase in number of COVID-19 confirmed cases. Considering these features, we are aimed at quantifying this multi-dimensional relationship by modelling change (predicted - actual) in electricity demand using a Vector Autoregressive Model (VAR). Analysis of variance decomposition in VAR model indicates the percentage contribution of the above mentioned factors to change in electricity demand. In NSW, social distancing seems to account for significant proportion of the change in electricity demand during COVID months.

Impulse Response Function

Let’s look at the impulse response function to understand the sensitivity of COVID-19 related factors to the change in electricity demand. The fluctuations in the impulse response is the representative of sensitivity to electricity change with respect to change in factors. Victoria represents that the change in electricity demand is most sensitive to change in the Melbourne pedestrian count with fluctuations in count lasting longer. Further, number of confirmed cases also does not seem to be much stronger influencing factor to the change in electricity demand.

Further, despite showing a larger contribution to the change in electricity demand during FEVD analysis, it is not necessary that the factor will provide high sensitivity in impulse response analysis. For instance, in NSW, number of confirmed seems to contribute a significant share to the change in electricity demand but shows very little sensitivity to the change in electricity demand in impulse response analysis. Thus, any measure alone cannot be used to assess the impact on change in electricity demand but both measure taken together can be used to measure the influence.

Conclusion

Our findings are well able to measure the dynamics between number of COVID confirmed cases, people’s changing behaviour and government policies across different states in different time scales Thus we conclude that traditional forecasting methods must be used together with such multi-dimensional analysis to assess the impact the demand of electricity during uncertain times such as COVID-19.
